Tourism in Australia

An interactive dashboard exploring the economic impact of tourism in Australia, focusing on key trends before and after the COVID-19 downturn. It highlights the role of domestic and international travel, changes in industry contribution, and overall economic recovery using multiple data visualizations.

Overnight Tourism Trips in Australia (2024)

Overnight tourism in Australia is heavily concentrated in major population centres, with New South Wales and Victoria attracting the highest visitor numbers.

Domestic travel remains the backbone of Australia’s tourism industry. Strong transport networks, major events, and diverse attractions in metropolitan and regional hubs continue to drive demand. Queensland and Western Australia are also key contributors to national tourism activity.

The gradual reopening of borders has reignited inbound tourism, with Sydney and Melbourne serving as key entry points. Queensland has shown particularly strong growth, reflecting its popularity among leisure travellers. This recovery plays a crucial role in boosting tourism expenditure and supporting the wider economy.

Change in Gross Value Added by Tourism Industry

Growth in tourism’s economic contribution has been strongest in education, food services, and accommodation industries.

Tourism vs Whole Economy GDP Over Time

Tourism’s contribution to GDP fell sharply during the COVID-19 downturn but has since rebounded more rapidly than the broader economy.

The return of international students, high domestic travel demand, and renewed confidence in hospitality have driven significant increases in GVA across key tourism sectors. Transport and retail services have also benefited from increased mobility and visitor spending.

The 2020 decline reflected international border closures and restrictions on movement. Recovery has been driven by domestic travel, followed by accelerating inbound visitation since 2022. The sector is now playing a growing role in Australia’s post-pandemic economic performance.

Strong domestic demand has sustained the sector through periods of volatility, while inbound travel has contributed to renewed growth since border restrictions eased. This dual recovery highlights tourism’s resilience and its role as a key economic driver.
